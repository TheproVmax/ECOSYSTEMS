🏞️ Lakes

A lake ecosystem is a community of living organisms (biotic factors) that interact with each other and their non-living surroundings (abiotic factors) in a still body of freshwater, such as a lake or pond. These ecosystems are typically divided into zones based on depth and light penetration. The top layer (littoral zone) near the shore is shallow, sunny, and rich with life, including plants like water lilies, reeds, and animals like frogs, turtles, and insects. The deeper, open water area (limnetic zone) receives enough sunlight for algae to grow, providing food for fish.

Living creatures in a lake are part of a complex food web. Primary producers, such as algae and phytoplankton, use sunlight and nutrients to create food, forming the foundation of the ecosystem. These are eaten by tiny zooplankton and small fish, which in turn are eaten by larger fish, birds like herons, and mammals. Decomposers, such as bacteria, live on the lake bottom, breaking down dead plants and animals to recycle nutrients back into the water.

The health of a lake ecosystem is determined by non-living factors like water temperature, dissolved oxygen levels, and nutrients like phosphorus. Lakes serve as vital habitats, providing breeding and nesting spots for animals and water for the surrounding land. However, lakes are sensitive to pollution and can slowly shrink and transform into marshes over thousands of years as they fill with sediment.
